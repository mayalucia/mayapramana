This document is the master curriculum for MāyāPramāṇa's lesson sequence. ** Pedagogical Principles

*** From Landau

Landau's /Course of Theoretical Physics/ embodies a principle we adopt: /every result is derived, not stated/. No equation falls from the sky. Each derivation starts from something already established --- an axiom, a previous result, or a physical argument. The student never has to accept a claim on authority alone.

- *No result without derivation*
- *Derivations start from established ground*
- *Physical arguments before algebraic manipulation*
- *Dimensional analysis as sanity check at every stage*
- *Order-of-magnitude estimates before exact solutions*

*** From Kip Thorne

Thorne's /Modern Classical Physics/ demonstrates that physical intuition can be taught --- through geometric reasoning, through cross-domain connections, through the habit of asking "what happens in the limit?" before computing.

- *Geometric/pictorial reasoning* whenever possible (Bloch sphere, phase space, Bode plots, information geometry)
- *Cross-domain connections made explicit*: the same Bloch equations in NMR, MRI, and atomic clocks; the same Kalman filter in magnetometry and navigation
- *Physical intuition questions embedded*: "What happens if $T_2 \to 0$? What does that mean for the atoms?"
- *Information-theoretic thread*: Fisher information connects noise (Lesson 05) → estimation (Lesson 07) → control (Lesson 08)

*** From the MāyāPramāṇa Manifesto

- *Feynman Imperative*: what I cannot create, I do not understand. Every lesson produces running code.
- *Three languages, one physics*: Python (intuition), Haskell (specification), C++ (deployment). All must agree.
- *Tests are specifications*: "Bloch norm conserved under pure precession", not "test_bloch_3".
- *Pramāṇa framework*: pratyakṣa (the raw signal), anumāna (the inference from it), upamāna (one sensor teaches us about another).

*** What This Lesson Covers

The Bloch equations as the dynamical foundation:

$$\frac{d\mathbf{M}}{dt} = \gamma \mathbf{M} \times \mathbf{B}
  - \begin{pmatrix} M_x / T_2 \\ M_y / T_2 \\ (M_z - M_0) / T_1 \end{pmatrix}$$

- The Bloch vector as a parametrization of the spin-½ density matrix
- Pure precession ($\gamma \mathbf{M} \times \mathbf{B}$) as unitary evolution
- Transverse relaxation ($T_2$): decoherence, loss of phase information
- Longitudinal relaxation ($T_1$): thermalisation toward equilibrium
- Numerical integration: RK4 as a state transformation
- Simulation as a fold (=scanl=) over time

*** Key Equations

| Equation | Physical meaning |
|----------+------------------|
| $\omega_L = \gamma \vert\mathbf{B}\vert$ | Larmor frequency |
| $\gamma_F = g_F \mu_B / \hbar$ | Effective gyromagnetic ratio |
| $\rho = \frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{I} + \mathbf{M} \cdot \boldsymbol{\sigma})$ | Density matrix ↔ Bloch vector |

*** Verification Claims

- "Bloch norm conserved under pure precession" ($T_1, T_2 \to \infty$)
- "Spin returns to initial state after one Larmor period"
- "Longitudinal relaxation reaches $M_0$ within $e^{-5}$ after $5T_1$"
- "Transverse envelope decays as $e^{-t/T_2}$"

** Lesson 01 — Angular Momentum and the Quantum Spin

#+begin_quote
/Everyone who has learned about the spinning electron knows that
the most basic properties of the electron can't be described by
classical mechanics. So the student is told to forget about it.
But it's not possible to forget about it, as long as the
mathematical equations contain the image of a spinning sphere./
--- Lev Landau, Quantum Mechanics
#+end_quote

*** Why This Lesson

Lesson 00 /uses/ the Bloch vector but doesn't derive /why/ spin is
quantized. The graduate student who has read Griffiths QM has seen
spin-½ introduced as a postulate ("there exists an intrinsic angular
momentum with $s = 1/2$"). This lesson shows that quantization
/emerges from the algebra of angular momentum/ --- it is not a
separate postulate but a consequence of the commutation relations.

Without this lesson, the student treats the Bloch equations as
phenomenological. With it, they see that the Bloch vector is the
density matrix in disguise, and that the density matrix is the
natural language for a spin coupled to its environment.

*** Physics Progression

1. *Classical angular momentum as a generator of rotation*
   - $\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{p}$, but more deeply:
     $\{L_i, f\} = $ rotation of $f$ about axis $i$
   - Poisson bracket $\{L_i, L_j\} = \epsilon_{ijk} L_k$
   - Angular momentum generates the rotation group SO(3)

2. *From classical to quantum: the commutation relations*
   - Canonical quantization: $\{,\} \to \frac{1}{i\hbar}[,]$
   - $[J_i, J_j] = i\hbar \epsilon_{ijk} J_k$
   - These relations alone — without any reference to orbits or
     wavefunctions — determine everything

3. *The algebra forces quantization*
   - Ladder operators $J_\pm = J_x \pm i J_y$
   - $[J_z, J_\pm] = \pm\hbar J_\pm$ (raising/lowering by one unit)
   - $[J_+, J_-] = 2\hbar J_z$
   - The algebra closes only if $j = 0, \frac{1}{2}, 1, \frac{3}{2}, \ldots$
   - Half-integer values: intrinsic angular momentum (spin) — not
     orbits, not classical, forced by the mathematics

4. *Spin-½: the Pauli matrices*
   - The $j = 1/2$ representation: $\mathbf{J} = \frac{\hbar}{2}\boldsymbol{\sigma}$
   - $\sigma_x, \sigma_y, \sigma_z$ as generators of SU(2)
   - Why SU(2), not SO(3)? The double cover — rotate $2\pi$ and the
     state picks up a minus sign. Spinors are not vectors.

5. *The density matrix: why pure states are not enough*
   - A spin in thermal equilibrium: $\rho = e^{-H/k_BT} / Z$
   - A spin entangled with its environment: partial trace → mixed state
   - The density matrix $\rho$ is the honest description of a
     quantum system that interacts with a bath
   - Properties: Hermitian, unit trace, positive semidefinite
   - Pure state: $\rho^2 = \rho$, $\text{tr}(\rho^2) = 1$
   - Mixed state: $\text{tr}(\rho^2) < 1$

6. *Back to Bloch: the derivation*
   - A $2 \times 2$ Hermitian matrix with unit trace has 3 real
     parameters → $\rho = \frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{I} + \mathbf{M} \cdot \boldsymbol{\sigma})$
   - $M_i = \text{tr}(\rho \sigma_i)$ — the Bloch vector is the
     expectation value of the Pauli matrices
   - $|\mathbf{M}| = 1$: pure state (surface of Bloch sphere)
   - $|\mathbf{M}| < 1$: mixed state (interior)
   - Von Neumann equation $i\hbar \dot{\rho} = [H, \rho]$ with
     $H = -\frac{\gamma\hbar}{2}\boldsymbol{\sigma} \cdot \mathbf{B}$
     → the precession term of Lesson 00
   - Lindblad dissipator → the relaxation terms of Lesson 00
   - *The Bloch equations are now derived, not postulated*

*** Key Equations

| Equation | Physical meaning |
|----------+------------------|
| $[J_i, J_j] = i\hbar \epsilon_{ijk} J_k$ | Angular momentum algebra |
| $J^2 \vert j,m\rangle = \hbar^2 j(j+1) \vert j,m\rangle$ | Quantization of angular momentum |
| $J_\pm \vert j,m\rangle = \hbar\sqrt{j(j+1)-m(m\pm 1)} \vert j,m\pm 1\rangle$ | Ladder operators |
| $\rho = \frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{I} + \mathbf{M} \cdot \boldsymbol{\sigma})$ | Density matrix ↔ Bloch vector |
| $i\hbar\dot{\rho} = [H, \rho]$ | Von Neumann equation |
| $\dot{\rho} = -\frac{i}{\hbar}[H,\rho] + \mathcal{L}[\rho]$ | Lindblad master equation |

*** Computational Content

- Python :: Construct Pauli matrices, compute commutators,
  demonstrate $[J_i, J_j] = i\hbar\epsilon_{ijk}J_k$ numerically.
  Build a density matrix from a Bloch vector, check purity. Visualise
  the Bloch sphere with a state trajectory.
- Haskell :: =DensityMatrix= type with smart constructor enforcing
  Hermiticity and unit trace. Algebraic verification of angular
  momentum commutation relations. Property: rotating a pure state
  preserves purity.
- C++ :: =constexpr= Pauli matrices. Compile-time verification that
  $\sigma_i \sigma_j = \delta_{ij} I + i\epsilon_{ijk}\sigma_k$.

*** Verification Claims

- "Angular momentum commutation relations close"
- "Ladder operators produce correct Clebsch-Gordan coefficients for $j = 1/2$"
- "Density matrix from Bloch vector is Hermitian, unit trace, positive semidefinite"
- "Von Neumann equation reproduces Bloch precession"
- "Lindblad dissipator reproduces $T_1, T_2$ relaxation"

*** Cadenzas

- =stern-gerlach=: The experiment that forced quantization. For
  students who haven't seen it or have forgotten.
- =su2-vs-so3=: Why spinors need a double cover. Physical
  consequences: the neutron interferometry experiment, the Berry

- =density-matrix-motivation=: Why not just use state vectors? The

  system you can't isolate.

*** Exercises

1. *Spin-1 Bloch vector*: Generalise to $j = 1$. The density matrix
   is $3\times 3$. How many real parameters? What replaces the Bloch
   sphere?
2. *Thermal equilibrium*: Compute $\rho$ for a spin-½ in a field

   What is $M_0$ (the equilibrium magnetization in the Bloch

3. *Decoherence*: Starting from a pure state $|\uparrow_x\rangle$,

   decoherence.

** Lesson 02 — Atom-Light Interaction

:STATUS: PLANNED

#+begin_quote
/The career of a young theoretical physicist consists of treating the
harmonic oscillator in ever-increasing levels of abstraction./
--- Sidney Coleman
#+end_quote

*** Why This Lesson

The magnetometer uses a laser to both /prepare/ (optical pumping) and
/read out/ (Faraday rotation) the atomic spin state. The student must
understand how electromagnetic radiation drives atomic transitions ---
not at the level of Fermi's golden rule (a rate, coarse-grained) but
at the level of Rabi oscillations (coherent dynamics, fine-grained).

This is where the Bloch equations of Lesson 00 gain a /source term/:
the driving field. And where the two-level atom --- the simplest
quantum system --- meets the classical electromagnetic field.

*** Physics Progression

1. *The electric dipole approximation*
   - Full interaction: $H' = -e\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{E}(t)$
   - Why $\mathbf{E} \cdot \mathbf{r}$ and not the full
     $\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{p}$: wavelength >> atomic size
   - Dipole matrix element $\mathbf{d}_{12} = e\langle 1|\mathbf{r}|2\rangle$

2. *Selection rules from angular momentum*
   - The photon carries angular momentum $\ell = 1$
   - $\Delta m = 0$ (linear, $\pi$), $\pm 1$ (circular, $\sigma^\pm$)
   - Triangle rule: $|j_1 - 1| \leq j_2 \leq j_1 + 1$
   - Parity: electric dipole → parity must change
   - This is the payoff of Lesson 01's angular momentum algebra

3. *Two-level atom in a classical field: Rabi oscillations*
   - Hamiltonian: $H = \frac{\hbar\omega_0}{2}\sigma_z + \frac{\hbar\Omega}{2}(\sigma_+ e^{-i\omega t} + \sigma_- e^{i\omega t})$
   - Rabi frequency: $\Omega = \mathbf{d}_{12} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 / \hbar$
   - Exact solution: oscillation between $|1\rangle$ and $|2\rangle$
     at the generalised Rabi frequency
     $\tilde{\Omega} = \sqrt{\Omega^2 + \Delta^2}$
   - $\pi$-pulse: complete inversion. $\pi/2$-pulse: equal
     superposition.

4. *The rotating wave approximation (RWA)*
   - Near resonance ($|\Delta| \ll \omega$), counter-rotating terms
     average out
   - Validity criterion: $\Omega \ll \omega_0$
   - What breaks down beyond the RWA: Bloch-Siegert shift

5. *Dressed states*
   - Diagonalise $H$ in the rotating frame → avoided crossing
   - The atom-field system as a single entity
   - Autler-Townes splitting: the spectroscopic signature

6. *Spontaneous emission*
   - Physical argument: the vacuum fluctuations drive decay
   - Einstein $A$ coefficient: $A_{21} = \omega_0^3 |\mathbf{d}_{12}|^2 / (3\pi\epsilon_0\hbar c^3)$
   - Lifetime $\tau = 1/A_{21}$; for Rb D2 line: $\tau \approx 26$ ns
   - Weisskopf-Wigner: exponential decay of the excited state

7. *The optical Bloch equations*
   - Bloch equations (Lesson 00) + spontaneous emission (this lesson)
   - Three components: precession (coherent drive), dephasing
     ($\gamma_2 = A_{21}/2 + \gamma_\text{coll}$), decay ($\gamma_1 = A_{21}$)
   - Steady-state solution: saturation, power broadening
   - The connection: Lesson 00's phenomenological $T_1, T_2$ now have
     microscopic content

*** Key Equations

| Equation | Physical meaning |
|----------+------------------|
| $\Omega = \mathbf{d}_{12} \cdot \mathbf{E}_0 / \hbar$ | Rabi frequency (coupling strength) |
| $\tilde{\Omega} = \sqrt{\Omega^2 + \Delta^2}$ | Generalised Rabi frequency (includes detuning) |
| $P_2(t) = \frac{\Omega^2}{\tilde{\Omega}^2}\sin^2(\tilde{\Omega}t/2)$ | Rabi oscillation (excitation probability) |
| $A_{21} = \frac{\omega_0^3 |\mathbf{d}_{12}|^2}{3\pi\epsilon_0\hbar c^3}$ | Spontaneous emission rate |

*** Verification Claims

- "Rabi oscillation period matches $2\pi/\tilde{\Omega}$"
- "$\pi$-pulse achieves complete population inversion"
- "Steady-state population saturates at $1/2$ for $\Omega \gg \Gamma$"
- "Optical Bloch equations reduce to Lesson 00 when drive is off"

*** Cadenzas

- =dipole-matrix-elements=: How to compute
  $\langle n'l'm'|\mathbf{r}|nlm\rangle$ for hydrogen-like atoms.
  Radial and angular parts. Wigner-Eckart theorem (statement +
  physical meaning, not proof).
- =time-dependent-perturbation-review=: Fermi's golden rule as a
  coarse-grained limit of Rabi oscillations. When is the rate
  description valid?
- =semiclassical-vs-quantum-field=: We treat the laser classically.
  When does this break down? Jaynes-Cummings model preview.

*** Exercises

1. *Off-resonant Rabi*: Plot $P_2(t)$ for $\Delta = 0, \Omega, 3\Omega$.
   Observe: off-resonance, the oscillation is faster but incomplete.
2. *Optical Bloch equations*: Simulate the OBE for a two-level atom
   driven by a laser that turns on at $t = 0$. Observe transient Rabi
   oscillations damping to steady state.
3. *Saturation parameter*: Define $s = \Omega^2 / (2\Delta^2 + \Gamma^2/2)$.
   Show that the steady-state excited population is
   $\rho_{22} = \frac{s/2}{1+s}$. What does $s \gg 1$ mean physically?

** Lesson 03 — Optical Pumping

#+begin_quote
/The purpose of the optical pumping method is to produce a
macroscopic orientation of atomic angular momentum, starting
from a random, thermal equilibrium distribution./
--- Alfred Kastler, Nobel Lecture, 1966
#+end_quote

*** Why This Lesson

Optical pumping is how the magnetometer is /prepared/. Without it,
the spin states are equally populated (at room temperature,
$k_BT \gg g_F \mu_B B$), the net magnetization is negligible, and
there is no signal. Optical pumping creates the $M_0$ that appears
in Lesson 00's Bloch equations.

This is the first lesson where we confront the real atom --- not a
two-level abstraction but $^{87}$Rb with its hyperfine structure,
Zeeman sublevels, and selection rules.

*** Physics Progression

1. *The multi-level atom: hyperfine structure of $^{87}$Rb*
   - Nuclear spin $I = 3/2$, electron spin $S = 1/2$ for ground state
   - Hyperfine coupling: $\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{I} + \mathbf{J}$
   - Ground state: $F = 1$ (3 sublevels) and $F = 2$ (5 sublevels)
   - Excited state ($5P_{3/2}$): $F' = 0, 1, 2, 3$
   - Energy scales: hyperfine splitting $\sim 6.8$ GHz (ground state),
     Zeeman splitting $\sim 700$ kHz/G

2. *Circularly polarized light and selection rules*
   - $\sigma^+$ light: $\Delta m_F = +1$
   - Each absorption event pushes population toward higher $m_F$
   - Spontaneous emission: $\Delta m_F = 0, \pm 1$ (isotropic)
   - Net effect: population accumulates in the stretched state
     $|F=2, m_F = +2\rangle$

3. *Rate equations: population dynamics*
   - $\dot{P}_{m_F} = \sum_{m_F'} (R_{m_F' \to m_F} P_{m_F'} - R_{m_F \to m_F'} P_{m_F})$
   - Pumping rates $R \propto |C_{m_F, m_F'}|^2 \cdot I / I_\text{sat}$
   - Clebsch-Gordan coefficients determine the branching ratios
   - This is a system of coupled linear ODEs — the same =simulate=
     structure as Lesson 00, but for N populations instead of 3 Bloch
     components

4. *Dark states*
   - The stretched state $|F=2, m_F=+2\rangle$ with $\sigma^+$ light:
     no transition to $F'=3, m_F'=+3$ exists (for D1 line); the atom
     stops absorbing
   - Coherent dark states (CPT): superpositions that decouple from
     the light field
   - Population trapping: why optical pumping to a dark state is
     efficient

5. *Repumping*
   - Population in $F=1$ is "lost" to pumping on the $F=2$ transition
   - Second laser on $F=1 \to F'=2$ recycles atoms back to $F=2$
   - Without repumper: signal decays. With repumper: steady state.

6. *Equilibrium polarization: the steady-state $M_0$*
   - Balance: pumping rate (pushes toward $m_F = F$) vs relaxation
     rate (randomises $m_F$)
   - $M_0 = \langle F_z \rangle / F = f(\text{pump rate}, \text{relax rate})$
   - This is the $M_0$ in the Bloch equations of Lesson 00 — now
     derived from microscopic physics

7. *Optical pumping time*
   - $\tau_\text{pump} \sim 1/(\Gamma \cdot s)$ where $s$ is the
     saturation parameter
   - For Rb: $\tau_\text{pump} \sim 10\text{--}100\,\mu$s depending
     on intensity
   - The competition: $\tau_\text{pump}$ vs $T_1$. If pumping is
     slower than relaxation, polarization is poor.

*** Key Equations

| Equation | Physical meaning |
|----------+------------------|
| $\dot{P}_{m_F} = \sum (R_{in} - R_{out})$ | Rate equation for sublevel populations |
| $R \propto \vert C_{m_F, m_F'}\vert^2 I/I_\text{sat}$ | Pumping rate (proportional to CG coefficients × intensity) |
| $M_0 = \langle F_z \rangle / F$ | Equilibrium polarization |
| $\tau_\text{pump} \sim 1/(\Gamma \cdot s)$ | Optical pumping time |

*** Verification Claims

- "Rate equations conserve total population: $\sum P_{m_F} = 1$"
- "Pumping with $\sigma^+$ drives population toward $m_F = +F$"
- "Equilibrium polarization increases monotonically with pump rate"
- "Repumping prevents population loss to $F=1$"

*** Cadenzas

- =hyperfine-structure-alkali=: The hierarchy of atomic energy scales
  (fine structure → hyperfine → Zeeman). How to read a Grotrian
  diagram.
- =clebsch-gordan-coefficients=: What they are, where they come from,
  how to look them up. Enough to compute branching ratios.
- =rate-vs-master-equation=: When rate equations suffice (incoherent
  processes dominate) and when you need the full Lindblad master

** Lesson 04 — Magnetic Resonance

:STATUS: PLANNED
:FILE: lessons/04-magnetic-resonance/concept.org

#+begin_quote
/I am what is mine. Personality is the original personal property./
--- Norman O. Brown

/The Larmor frequency is the atom's personal property. It tells you
the magnetic field it feels, and no other./
--- (the physics, paraphrased)
#+end_quote

*** Why This Lesson

This is the lesson where the magnetometer /works/. The student learns
how the Larmor precession frequency — established in Lesson 00 and
underpinned by the angular momentum algebra of Lesson 01 — becomes a
measurement of the magnetic field. The Bell-Bloom scheme, which is the
specific technique used in Saikat's magnetometer, is developed here.

*** Physics Progression

1. *Zeeman splitting*
   - In weak field: $E_{m_F} = g_F \mu_B B m_F$ (linear Zeeman)
   - Level spacing: $\Delta E = g_F \mu_B B = \hbar \omega_L$
   - For $^{87}$Rb $F=2$: $g_F \approx 1/2$, so
     $\omega_L / 2\pi \approx 7\,\text{Hz/nT}$
   - This is the conversion factor that appears in the UQSC prototype

2. *The resonance condition*
   - An oscillating field at frequency $\omega$ near $\omega_L$ drives
     transitions between Zeeman sublevels
   - On resonance ($\omega = \omega_L$): maximum transition rate
   - Off resonance: reduced rate, oscillation at
     $\sqrt{(\omega - \omega_L)^2 + \Omega_\text{rf}^2}$
   - $\omega_L = \gamma |B|$ is the /fundamental equation of
     magnetometry/

3. *rf-driven transitions: Rabi oscillations in the Zeeman manifold*
   - Same mathematics as Lesson 02, but between $m_F$ and $m_F \pm 1$
     instead of ground and excited states
   - Rotating frame: the Bloch equations in a frame rotating at
     $\omega$, with detuning $\delta = \omega - \omega_L$
   - On resonance in the rotating frame: pure nutation about $B_1$

4. *The Bell-Bloom scheme*
   - Modulated optical pumping: the pump beam is amplitude-modulated
     at a frequency near $\omega_L$
   - Each pump pulse creates a burst of transverse magnetization
   - If the modulation frequency matches $\omega_L$, successive pulses
     add coherently → large steady-state oscillation
   - If it doesn't match: destructive interference → small signal
   - The signal amplitude as a function of modulation frequency → the
     magnetic resonance lineshape

5. *Free induction decay (FID)*
   - After a single pump pulse, the transverse magnetization
     oscillates at $\omega_L$ and decays with $T_2$
   - This is the transient signal from Lesson 00, now in context
   - FFT of the FID → the Larmor frequency → the magnetic field

6. *Continuous-wave resonance: the lineshape*
   - Absorptive (Lorentzian) component: proportional to power absorbed
   - Dispersive component: proportional to $\delta / (\delta^2 + \gamma^2)$
   - The dispersive signal crosses zero at resonance with a steep
     slope — /this is the error signal/

7. *The dispersive signal as an error signal: DAVLL physics*
   - In DAVLL: Zeeman-split absorption produces a differential signal
     between $\sigma^+$ and $\sigma^-$ components
   - This differential signal is dispersive: zero at resonance,
     positive on one side, negative on the other
   - Feed it to a PID → the laser stays locked to the atomic
     resonance
   - This is the physics behind RP1 in the UQSC prototype

*** Key Equations

| Equation | Physical meaning |
|----------+------------------|
| $E_{m_F} = g_F \mu_B B m_F$ | Linear Zeeman splitting |
| $\omega_L = \gamma \vert B \vert$ | Larmor frequency (the measurement equation) |
| $B = f_L / (\gamma/2\pi)$ | Field from Larmor frequency |
| $\chi(\delta) \propto \frac{\gamma}{\delta^2 + \gamma^2} + i\frac{\delta}{\delta^2 + \gamma^2}$ | Complex susceptibility (absorptive + dispersive) |

- "FID frequency extracted by FFT agrees with $\omega_L$"

- "DAVLL error signal has correct sign for negative feedback"

** Lesson 05 — Noise and Sensitivity Limits
:PROPERTIES:
:STATUS: PLANNED
:FILE: lessons/05-noise-sensitivity/concept.org
:DEPENDS: 00, 01, 03, 04
:EXPORTS: ShotNoise, JohnsonNoise, QuantumProjectionNoise, CramerRaoBound, AllanDeviation, noise_budget
:END:

#+begin_quote
/Noise is the body of the signal. Without it, you are hearing ghosts./
--- (paraphrasing Wiener)
#+end_quote

*** Why This Lesson

Before the student can process signals (Lesson 06) or design
estimators (Lesson 07), they must understand /what noise is/ and /why
there are fundamental limits to measurement precision/. This lesson
is the bridge between physics (what the sensor does) and engineering
(what we do about it).

The Cramér-Rao bound — the speed limit of measurement — threads
through the rest of the curriculum.

*** Physics Progression

1. *Statistical mechanics of fluctuations*
   - Fluctuation-dissipation theorem: any system that dissipates
     energy also fluctuates. Landau-style derivation from the
     partition function.
   - The connection: a resistor that produces Johnson noise is the
     /same/ dissipation that damps the signal

2. *Shot noise*: $\delta N = \sqrt{N}$
   - Poisson statistics of photon counting
   - For $N$ detected photons: $\text{SNR} = \sqrt{N}$
   - Optical power → photon rate → shot noise floor
   - This sets the /photon-limited/ sensitivity of the readout

3. *Johnson-Nyquist noise*: $v_n = \sqrt{4 k_B T R \Delta f}$
   - Thermal fluctuations in the electronics
   - Voltage noise spectral density: $S_v = 4 k_B T R$
   - Dominates at low frequencies in transimpedance amplifiers

4. *Quantum projection noise*
   - Fundamental quantum limit for $N$ spin-½ particles:
     $\delta B = \frac{1}{\gamma \sqrt{N T_2 t}}$
   - Origin: the uncertainty principle for angular momentum
     measurements. You cannot know $J_z$ perfectly and also have
     coherence in $J_x, J_y$.
   - The /standard quantum limit/ (SQL) for atomic magnetometry
   - For Rb: $N \sim 10^{10}$ atoms, $T_2 \sim 10$ ms → achievable
     sensitivities in the fT/$\sqrt{\text{Hz}}$ range

5. *Spin-exchange broadening*
   - Atom-atom collisions that randomize the spin direction → $T_2$
     broadening → worse sensitivity
   - Spin-exchange rate: $\propto n \sigma v_\text{th}$ (density ×
     cross-section × thermal velocity)
   - The nemesis of high-density vapor cells

6. *The SERF regime*
   - At zero field: spin-exchange collisions become spin-preserving
     (technically: $\omega_L T_\text{SE} \ll 1$)
   - $T_2$ increases dramatically → sensitivity improves by orders
     of magnitude
   - SERF magnetometers: sub-fT/$\sqrt{\text{Hz}}$, the most
     sensitive magnetometers in existence

7. *The Cramér-Rao bound and Fisher information*
   - The estimation problem: given noisy data $y$, estimate $B$
   - Fisher information: $\mathcal{I}(B) = E\left[\left(\frac{\partial \ln p(y|B)}{\partial B}\right)^2\right]$
   - Cramér-Rao inequality: $\text{Var}(\hat{B}) \geq 1/\mathcal{I}(B)$
   - Physical meaning: the curvature of the likelihood tells you how
     distinguishable nearby field values are
   - Connection to quantum mechanics: quantum Fisher information
     bounds what /any/ measurement can achieve

8. *Allan deviation*
   - Characterising noise as a function of averaging time $\tau$
   - White noise: $\sigma(\tau) \propto \tau^{-1/2}$
   - Flicker (1/f) noise: $\sigma(\tau) \propto$ const
   - Random walk: $\sigma(\tau) \propto \tau^{1/2}$
   - The Allan deviation plot reveals the noise type and the optimal
     averaging time

9. *Noise budget: putting it all together*
   - For a specific magnetometer (Bell-Bloom, $^{87}$Rb, room temp):
     tabulate shot noise, Johnson noise, QPN, spin-exchange
   - Which dominates? At what frequency? At what integration time?
   - The total sensitivity: $\delta B_\text{total} = \sqrt{\sum_i (\delta B_i)^2}$

*** Key Equations

| Equation | Physical meaning |
|----------+------------------|
| $\delta N = \sqrt{N}$ | Shot noise (Poisson statistics) |
| $S_v = 4 k_B T R$ | Johnson noise spectral density |
| $\delta B = \frac{1}{\gamma\sqrt{N T_2 t}}$ | Quantum projection noise limit |
| $\text{Var}(\hat{B}) \geq 1/\mathcal{I}(B)$ | Cramér-Rao bound |
| $\sigma_y(\tau) = \sqrt{\frac{1}{2(M-1)} \sum (y_{i+1} - y_i)^2}$ | Allan deviation |

- "Quantum projection noise matches $1/(\gamma\sqrt{NT_2 t})$ for simulated spin ensemble"
- "Allan deviation of white noise has slope $-1/2$ on log-log plot"
- "Cramér-Rao bound achieved by maximum likelihood estimator on simulated Lorentzian line"

** Lesson 06 — Signal Processing
:PROPERTIES:
:STATUS: PLANNED
:FILE: lessons/06-signal-processing/concept.org
:DEPENDS: 00, 04, 05
:EXPORTS: LockInAmplifier, DigitalFilter, FFT, PSD, fir_filter, iir_filter, lock_in_detect, quadrature_demod
:END:

#+begin_quote
/There is an essential difference between an analog signal
processor, which is a physical system obeying the laws of physics,
and a digital signal processor, which is a mathematical abstraction
implemented in silicon. The analog processor cannot violate
causality. The digital one can — it just looks into the future by
buffering the past./
#+end_quote

*** Why This Lesson

The magnetometer produces a signal: an oscillating voltage at the
Larmor frequency, buried in noise. This lesson teaches the tools to
extract it. Every block in the UQSC prototype's FPGA — IQ
demodulator, PID, ASG, IIR filter — implements one of the
mathematical operations taught here.

*** Physics Progression

1. *Sampling theory*
   - Nyquist-Shannon: $f_s > 2 f_\text{max}$, or aliasing occurs
   - Red Pitaya ADC: 125 MSPS, 14-bit → Nyquist at 62.5 MHz
   - Larmor frequency at Earth's field: ~350 kHz → well within Nyquist
   - Quantization noise: $\text{SNR}_\text{quant} = 6.02 N_\text{bits} + 1.76$ dB

2. *Digital filtering*
   - Convolution: $y[n] = \sum_k h[k] x[n-k]$
   - FIR filters: finite impulse response, always stable, linear
     phase available
   - IIR filters: infinite impulse response, more efficient but can be
     unstable. Butterworth, Chebyshev.
   - Frequency response: $H(e^{j\omega})$, gain and phase plots
   - Filter design as a pure function: coefficients → filter → output

3. *Lock-in detection: the heart of the magnetometer*
   - Multiply signal by reference: $s(t) \cos(\omega_\text{ref} t)$
   - Low-pass filter → extracts the component at $\omega_\text{ref}$
   - Result: amplitude $\times \cos(\phi)$ where $\phi$ is the phase
     difference
   - This is how the FPGA IQ module works

4. *Quadrature demodulation*
   - Two references: $\cos(\omega t)$ and $\sin(\omega t)$
   - Two outputs: $X = A\cos\phi$, $Y = A\sin\phi$
   - Amplitude: $A = \sqrt{X^2 + Y^2}$
   - Phase: $\phi = \arctan(Y/X)$
   - The full complex demodulation: $Z = X + iY = A e^{i\phi}$

5. *Spectral analysis*
   - DFT/FFT: $X[k] = \sum_n x[n] e^{-2\pi i kn/N}$
   - Power spectral density: $S_{xx}(f) = |X(f)|^2 / T$
   - Windowing: why rectangular is bad (spectral leakage), Hann, Hamming
   - Welch's method: overlapping segments for smooth PSD estimation

6. *Lock-in as optimal matched filter*
   - The lock-in amplifier extracts the signal at one frequency —
     exactly what a matched filter does when the signal model is a
     sinusoid at known frequency
   - SNR improvement: $\propto \sqrt{T_\text{integration}/T_\text{period}}$
   - Preview of Lesson 07: the Kalman filter as a generalisation of
     matched filtering to time-varying signals

*** Verification Claims

- "FIR filter frequency response matches design specification"
- "Lock-in detection recovers amplitude and phase of buried sinusoid to within noise floor"
- "FFT of simulated FID recovers Larmor frequency to within $1/T$ resolution"
- "PSD of white noise is flat; PSD of 1/f noise has slope $-1$"

** Lesson 07 — State Estimation
:PROPERTIES:
:STATUS: PLANNED
:FILE: lessons/07-state-estimation/concept.org
:DEPENDS: 05, 06
:EXPORTS: KalmanFilter, ExtendedKF, ParticleFilter, kalman_predict, kalman_update, fisher_information_matrix
:END:

#+begin_quote
/The Kalman filter does for estimation what PID does for control:
it solves the boring 90% of the problem, so you can focus on the
interesting 10%./
#+end_quote

*** Why This Lesson

The signal processing of Lesson 06 extracts a measurement. But a
single measurement is noisy. The magnetometer produces a /stream/ of
measurements, and the magnetic field is a /time-varying/ quantity. We
need an estimator that combines all past measurements optimally,
accounting for the dynamics of the field and the statistics of the
noise.

This is the Kalman filter — and its nonlinear extensions. It is the
"anumāna" (inference) engine of the magnetometer.

*** Physics Progression

1. *The estimation problem*
   - Hidden state: $\mathbf{x}_k$ (the magnetic field and its
     derivatives)
   - Observations: $\mathbf{y}_k$ (the demodulated Larmor signal)
   - Dynamics: $\mathbf{x}_{k+1} = \mathbf{A}\mathbf{x}_k + \mathbf{w}_k$
     (field changes + process noise)
   - Measurement: $\mathbf{y}_k = \mathbf{H}\mathbf{x}_k + \mathbf{v}_k$
     (observation + measurement noise)

2. *Maximum likelihood estimation*
   - The likelihood function: $p(\mathbf{y}|\mathbf{x})$
   - MLE: find $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ that maximizes $p$
   - For Gaussian noise: MLE = least squares
   - Connection to Fisher information (Lesson 05): the curvature of
     $\ln p$ at the MLE

3. *The linear Kalman filter*
   - Predict step: propagate state and covariance forward
   - Update step: incorporate new measurement, weight by Kalman gain
   - $K_k = P_k^- H^T (H P_k^- H^T + R)^{-1}$ (the Kalman gain)
   - The filter is /optimal/ for linear Gaussian systems (minimum
     variance unbiased estimator)
   - Derivation: from Bayesian updating of Gaussians (Landau-style:
     every step)

4. *The extended Kalman filter (EKF)*
   - Nonlinear dynamics: $\mathbf{x}_{k+1} = f(\mathbf{x}_k) + \mathbf{w}_k$
   - Linearize around current estimate: $F_k = \partial f / \partial \mathbf{x}|_{\hat{\mathbf{x}}_k}$
   - Same predict-update structure, but with Jacobians
   - When the EKF fails: highly nonlinear systems, multi-modal
     distributions

5. *Particle filters*
   - Represent the posterior $p(\mathbf{x}_k | \mathbf{y}_{1:k})$ as
     a set of weighted particles
   - No Gaussianity assumption, no linearization
   - Predict: propagate each particle through the dynamics
   - Update: reweight particles by likelihood of observation
   - Resample: prune unlikely particles, duplicate likely ones
   - When to use: multi-modal, highly nonlinear, non-Gaussian

6. *Sensor fusion*
   - Multiple sensors: magnetometer + IMU + altimeter (connection to
     MagNav document)
   - Each sensor provides a measurement equation
   - The Kalman filter combines them optimally
   - Loose coupling vs tight coupling

7. *Information form and Fisher information matrix*
   - The Kalman filter covariance $P_k$ is the inverse of the
     information matrix
   - Connection to Cramér-Rao bound (Lesson 05): the filter
     covariance converges to the Cramér-Rao bound
   - The filter is not just an algorithm — it is an /information
     processing/ system

*** Verification Claims

- "Kalman filter converges to true field in linear Gaussian simulation"
- "EKF tracks sinusoidally varying field with bounded error"
- "Particle filter captures bimodal distribution (two field hypotheses)"
- "Filter covariance converges to Cramér-Rao bound for stationary field"
- "Fusing two sensors gives lower variance than either alone"

** Lesson 08 — Feedback and Control
:PROPERTIES:
:STATUS: PLANNED
:FILE: lessons/08-feedback-control/concept.org
:DEPENDS: 04, 05, 06, 07
:EXPORTS: PIDController, TransferFunction, BodePlot, pid_step, bode_analysis, stability_margin, ziegler_nichols
:END:

#+begin_quote
/A feedback system is an argument with itself.
Good design ensures it's a productive argument./
#+end_quote

*** Why This Lesson

The magnetometer must /hold lock/ — keep the laser on resonance, keep
the PLL tracking the Larmor frequency, reject disturbances from
vibration, temperature drift, ambient field changes. This is feedback
control.

Saikat's UQSC prototype says "optimise PID gains" multiple times
without guidance. This lesson fills that gap — from the physics of
feedback to the practice of PID tuning.

*** Physics Progression

1. *The feedback loop*
   - Reference → error → controller → plant → output → sensor → error
   - Open-loop vs closed-loop: why feedback is necessary (the plant
     drifts, the world changes)
   - Negative feedback: the sign that ensures stability

2. *PID controller*
   - Proportional: reacts to current error. Proportional gain $K_p$.
   - Integral: eliminates steady-state error. Integral gain $K_i$.
   - Derivative: anticipates future error. Derivative gain $K_d$.
   - In the time domain: $u(t) = K_p e(t) + K_i \int e\,dt + K_d \dot{e}(t)$
   - Physical meaning: P → spring, I → gravity (pulls to zero), D → dashpot

3. *Transfer functions*
   - The Laplace transform: $\mathcal{L}\{f(t)\} = F(s) = \int_0^\infty f(t) e^{-st} dt$
   - Motivation: convolution → multiplication. Differential equations → algebra.
   - The PID in the $s$-domain: $C(s) = K_p + K_i/s + K_d s$
   - Plant transfer function: $G(s)$
   - Closed-loop: $H(s) = \frac{C(s)G(s)}{1 + C(s)G(s)}$

4. *Bode plots*
   - $|H(j\omega)|$ and $\angle H(j\omega)$ vs $\omega$
   - Gain margin: how much gain you can add before instability
   - Phase margin: how much phase lag you can tolerate
   - A Bode plot is a /portrait of the feedback loop's character/

5. *Stability*
   - The closed-loop is stable iff all poles have $\text{Re}(s) < 0$
   - Nyquist criterion: stability from the open-loop frequency response
   - Physical intuition: if the loop has gain > 1 at the frequency
     where phase = $-180°$, the feedback becomes /positive/ → oscillation

6. *Loop shaping*
   - Design the controller to meet bandwidth and stability specs
   - Bandwidth: the frequency up to which the loop tracks changes
   - Roll-off: the controller gain must decrease at high frequency
     (noise amplification)
   - Lead-lag compensation: trading bandwidth for stability

7. *Anti-windup*
   - The integral term accumulates error → if the actuator saturates
     (DAC at max), the integral keeps growing → when the error
     changes sign, the accumulated integral causes overshoot
   - Anti-windup: clamp the integral when the actuator saturates
   - This is the #1 practical PID bug, and Saikat's doc doesn't mention it

8. *PID tuning: Ziegler-Nichols and beyond*
   - Z-N ultimate gain method: increase $K_p$ until oscillation,
     measure period → compute $K_p, K_i, K_d$
   - Z-N step response method: from the plant's open-loop step response
   - Relay auto-tuning: automated Z-N
   - Beyond Z-N: Cohen-Coon, AMIGO, model-based tuning

9. *Feedback as estimation: the control-estimation duality*
   - The Kalman filter (Lesson 07) and the LQR controller are /duals/:
     same Riccati equation, transposed
   - The filter estimates the state; the controller decides the action
   - This duality is not an accident — it is the mathematical structure
     of optimal feedback under uncertainty

- "PID holds laser lock under simulated field step disturbance"
- "Bode plot phase margin > 45° for tuned controller"
- "Anti-windup prevents integral overshoot when DAC saturates"
- "Ziegler-Nichols tuning from simulated plant matches analytical prediction"
