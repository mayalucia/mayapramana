#+subtitle: Why does a quantum state live on a sphere?
#+LOOM: cadenza-expansion
#+CADENZA-OF: bloch-sphere-geometry
#+MODE: feynman

* The Encounter

Here is something that should bother you.

A quantum spin-½ state $|\psi\rangle = \alpha|{\uparrow}\rangle + \beta|{\downarrow}\rangle$ is described by two complex numbers, $\alpha$ and $\beta$. That is four real parameters. And yet the lesson just told us the Bloch vector $\mathbf{M} = (M_x, M_y, M_z)$ has three components and lives on a sphere. Where did the fourth parameter go? And why a /sphere/?

Let me try to figure this out.

** First attempt: just count

Two complex numbers: $\alpha = a + ib$, $\beta = c + id$. Four real numbers. But normalisation says $|\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1$, so that's one constraint. Three parameters left. Alright, three — that matches $\mathbb{R}^3$.

But wait. There's a subtlety I almost missed. An overall phase $|\psi\rangle \to e^{i\phi}|\psi\rangle$ doesn't change any physical observable — all expectation values $\langle\psi|A|\psi\rangle$ are unchanged. So we should quotient by the overall phase. That removes another parameter: 4 - 1 (normalisation) - 1 (global phase) = 2.

Two? Not three? That means the Bloch vector on the /surface/ of the
sphere has two degrees of freedom (the polar and azimuthal angles
$\theta$, $\phi$), and that is the space of pure states. The third
parameter — the radius $|\mathbf{M}|$ — appears only when we allow
mixed states (the density matrix). So the density matrix has 3
parameters, but a pure state has only 2.

That's satisfying. The sphere surface (2 parameters) = pure states. The sphere interior (3 parameters) = mixed states. The radius measures how mixed you are.

** Second attempt: build the map explicitly

Let me actually construct the Bloch vector from $|\psi\rangle$ and
see if it lands on the sphere.

Write $|\psi\rangle = \cos(\theta/2)|{\uparrow}\rangle + e^{i\phi}\sin(\theta/2)|{\downarrow}\rangle$
— this is the most general state after fixing normalisation and
removing global phase.

Now compute $M_i = \langle\psi|\sigma_i|\psi\rangle$:

$M_x = \langle\psi|\sigma_x|\psi\rangle$. Since $\sigma_x$ flips
the spin, this gives $2\mathrm{Re}(\alpha^*\beta) = \sin\theta\cos\phi$.

$M_y = \langle\psi|\sigma_y|\psi\rangle = 2\mathrm{Im}(\alpha^*\beta) = \sin\theta\sin\phi$.

$M_z = \langle\psi|\sigma_z|\psi\rangle = |\alpha|^2 - |\beta|^2 = \cos\theta$.

So $\mathbf{M} = (\sin\theta\cos\phi, \sin\theta\sin\phi, \cos\theta)$.
That is literally the parametrisation of a unit sphere in spherical
coordinates!

Here is what is remarkable. The angles $\theta$ and $\phi$ that
parametrise a quantum spin state are /exactly/ the spherical
coordinates of a point on a classical sphere. The quantum state /is/ a
direction in space. Not metaphorically — mathematically. The abstract
Hilbert space vector maps to a concrete direction you could point
your finger in.

** The thing that is not obvious

But here is the catch that tripped me up. The angle in the quantum
state is $\theta/2$, not $\theta$. If the Bloch vector points along
$+z$ ($\theta = 0$), the state is $|{\uparrow}\rangle$. If the Bloch
vector points along $-z$ ($\theta = \pi$), the state is
$|{\downarrow}\rangle$. To go from north pole to south pole, the
Bloch vector rotates by $\pi$ but the spinor rotates by $\pi/2$.

This is why a $2\pi$ rotation of the Bloch vector (which feels like
it should return you to the start) actually gives
$|\psi\rangle \to -|\psi\rangle$. You need a $4\pi$ rotation to get
back. The Bloch sphere is really SU(2), not SO(3) — a double cover.
For our purposes in the Bloch equations (which are equations for
$\mathbf{M}$, not for $|\psi\rangle$), this subtlety doesn't
matter. But it is there, and it is the reason spin-½ particles are
genuinely quantum: no classical object has this property.

** Now the mixed states

When the spin interacts with its environment — collisions with other
atoms, stray photons, collisions with the cell wall — the state
becomes entangled with the environment. If we only look at the spin
(tracing out the environment), the pure state on the surface
gets replaced by a mixed state /inside/ the sphere.

What does "inside" mean, physically? Not ignorance — not "we don't
know which pure state it is." It means the spin and the environment
are correlated, and no description of the spin alone can capture the
full state. The radius $|\mathbf{M}|$ is a measure of this entanglement.

$|\mathbf{M}| = 1$: the spin is in a definite quantum state. Pure.
$|\mathbf{M}| = 0$: the spin is maximally entangled with the environment. No information remains in the spin alone.

The $T_2$ decay in the Bloch equations is literally this: the
transverse components shrink, the Bloch vector moves inward, the spin
loses its phase information to the environment. Decoherence is a
geometric shrinking.

I find that extraordinary. A fundamentally quantum phenomenon —
entanglement with the environment — is visible as a simple geometric
fact: the point moves toward the centre of the sphere. The abstraction
of the Bloch sphere makes quantum decoherence /intuitive/.
