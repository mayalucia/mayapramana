* The Derivation

We establish that the Bloch sphere parametrisation is not a choice but
a consequence of the algebraic structure of 2×2 Hermitian matrices.

** The space of states

A density matrix $\rho$ for a spin-½ system is a 2×2 matrix
satisfying three constraints:

1. Hermitian: $\rho = \rho^\dagger$
2. Unit trace: $\mathrm{Tr}(\rho) = 1$
3. Positive semi-definite: $\rho \geq 0$

A general 2×2 complex matrix has 8 real parameters (2 per entry). The
Hermiticity constraint $\rho_{ij} = \rho_{ji}^*$ forces the diagonal
to be real and the off-diagonal elements to be conjugate pairs,
leaving 4 real parameters: $\rho_{11}$, $\rho_{22}$, $\mathrm{Re}(\rho_{12})$,
$\mathrm{Im}(\rho_{12})$. The unit trace constraint $\rho_{11} + \rho_{22} = 1$
removes one more, leaving 3.

Three real parameters. A point in $\mathbb{R}^3$.

** The Pauli basis

Any traceless Hermitian 2×2 matrix can be expanded in the Pauli
matrices $\sigma_x$, $\sigma_y$, $\sigma_z$:

$$\sigma_x = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad
  \sigma_y = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -i \\ i & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad
  \sigma_z = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}$$

This is a basis: any traceless Hermitian 2×2 matrix $A$ can be
uniquely written as $A = a_x \sigma_x + a_y \sigma_y + a_z \sigma_z$
with $a_x, a_y, a_z \in \mathbb{R}$. To verify completeness, note
that the space of traceless Hermitian 2×2 matrices is 3-dimensional
(same counting as above minus the trace), and the three Pauli matrices
are linearly independent (their traces against each other give the
orthogonality relation $\mathrm{Tr}(\sigma_i \sigma_j) = 2\delta_{ij}$).

Since $\rho$ is Hermitian with unit trace, we can write:

$$\rho = \frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{I} + \mathbf{M} \cdot \boldsymbol{\sigma})
       = \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 1 + M_z & M_x - iM_y \\ M_x + iM_y & 1 - M_z \end{pmatrix}$$

where $\mathbf{M} = (M_x, M_y, M_z)$ is the Bloch vector. The factor
$\frac{1}{2}\mathbf{I}$ accounts for the trace; the Bloch vector
captures the traceless part.

** Extracting $\mathbf{M}$ from $\rho$

Using the orthogonality relation $\mathrm{Tr}(\sigma_i \sigma_j) = 2\delta_{ij}$:

$$M_i = \mathrm{Tr}(\rho \, \sigma_i)$$

This is not a definition — it is a consequence of the basis expansion.
The components of $\mathbf{M}$ are expectation values of the Pauli
operators: $M_x = \langle \sigma_x \rangle$, $M_y = \langle \sigma_y \rangle$,
$M_z = \langle \sigma_z \rangle$.

** The constraint: $|\mathbf{M}| \leq 1$

The positive semi-definiteness of $\rho$ requires non-negative
eigenvalues. The eigenvalues of $\rho$ are:

$$\lambda_\pm = \frac{1}{2}(1 \pm |\mathbf{M}|)$$

(This follows from $\rho = \frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{I} + \mathbf{M} \cdot \boldsymbol{\sigma})$
and the fact that $\mathbf{M} \cdot \boldsymbol{\sigma}$ has
eigenvalues $\pm |\mathbf{M}|$, which is a standard result for Pauli
matrices: $(\mathbf{M} \cdot \boldsymbol{\sigma})^2 = |\mathbf{M}|^2 \mathbf{I}$.)

For $\lambda_- \geq 0$, we need $|\mathbf{M}| \leq 1$.

** Pure and mixed states

- $|\mathbf{M}| = 1$: one eigenvalue is 0, the other is 1. The
  density matrix is a rank-1 projector $\rho = |\psi\rangle\langle\psi|$.
  This is a /pure state/ — on the surface of the sphere.

- $|\mathbf{M}| < 1$: both eigenvalues are positive. This is a
  /mixed state/ — inside the sphere. The purity $\mathrm{Tr}(\rho^2) = \frac{1}{2}(1 + |\mathbf{M}|^2)$
  decreases as $|\mathbf{M}|$ decreases.

- $|\mathbf{M}| = 0$: $\rho = \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{I}$. The maximally
  mixed state — complete ignorance, the centre of the sphere.

The physical meaning of $|\mathbf{M}| < 1$ is not that we lack
knowledge of the state. It is that the spin is /entangled with its
environment/. The reduced density matrix of a subsystem in an
entangled state is necessarily mixed. Decoherence — the loss of phase
information to the environment — shrinks $|\mathbf{M}|$.

** Connection to the Bloch equations

The lesson defines $d\mathbf{M}/dt = \gamma \mathbf{M} \times \mathbf{B} - \text{relaxation}$.
In this language:

- /Precession/ ($\gamma \mathbf{M} \times \mathbf{B}$) rotates $\mathbf{M}$
  on a sphere of constant $|\mathbf{M}|$. It preserves purity.

- /Relaxation/ ($T_1$, $T_2$ terms) changes $|\mathbf{M}|$. Transverse
  relaxation ($T_2$) shrinks the transverse components, moving the
  state inward. Longitudinal relaxation ($T_1$) drives $M_z$ toward
  equilibrium $M_0$.

The Bloch equations are the equations of motion for a point on (or
inside) this sphere. The derivation of these equations from the von
Neumann equation $i\hbar \dot{\rho} = [H, \rho]$ plus a Lindblad
dissipator is the subject of Lesson 02.
