* The Picture

Before any algebra: what does a quantum spin-½ state /look like/?

** The sphere

Imagine a unit sphere in $\mathbb{R}^3$. The $z$-axis points up.

- The /north pole/ $(0, 0, 1)$ is spin-up: the state $|{\uparrow}\rangle$.
  If you measured $S_z$, you would always get $+\hbar/2$.

- The /south pole/ $(0, 0, -1)$ is spin-down: $|{\downarrow}\rangle$.
  Measurement of $S_z$ always gives $-\hbar/2$.

- The /equator/ is the set of states where $M_z = 0$. Measurement of
  $S_z$ gives $+\hbar/2$ or $-\hbar/2$ with equal probability. But
  the /phase/ between the two possibilities varies around the equator.
  The state pointing along $+x$ is
  $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|{\uparrow}\rangle + |{\downarrow}\rangle)$.
  The state pointing along $+y$ is
  $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|{\uparrow}\rangle + i|{\downarrow}\rangle)$.
  The equatorial angle /is/ the relative phase.

This is the Bloch sphere. Every point on its surface is a pure quantum
state. Every point inside is a mixed state — a statistical mixture, or
equivalently, a spin entangled with something else.

** The crucial picture: precession

Now turn on a magnetic field along $z$. The Bloch vector $\mathbf{M}$
precesses: it rotates about the $z$-axis at the Larmor frequency
$\omega_L = \gamma B_z$.

Picture this: a point on the surface of the sphere, tracing out a
circle of constant latitude. The polar angle $\theta$ (the tilt from
the $z$-axis) stays fixed — this means $M_z$ is constant. The
azimuthal angle $\phi$ increases uniformly: $\phi(t) = \phi_0 + \omega_L t$.

The transverse components oscillate: $M_x = \sin\theta\cos(\omega_L t)$,
$M_y = \sin\theta\sin(\omega_L t)$. This is the signal a
magnetometer reads — an oscillating transverse magnetisation whose
frequency encodes the field strength.

In the limit $\theta \to 0$ (spin nearly aligned with the field), the
circle shrinks to a point — no oscillation, no signal. In the limit
$\theta = \pi/2$ (spin in the equatorial plane), the circle is a
great circle — maximum signal amplitude.

** Relaxation as shrinking

Now add relaxation. $T_2$ decay damps the transverse components —
geometrically, the point spirals inward toward the $z$-axis. $T_1$
decay drives $M_z$ toward $M_0$ — geometrically, the point drifts
toward a target latitude.

The combined picture: a spiral that simultaneously wraps around the
$z$-axis (precession), tightens toward it ($T_2$), and drifts toward
a target height ($T_1$). The endpoint — after many $T_1$ times — is
the point $(0, 0, M_0)$ on the $z$-axis.

If $M_0 = 0$ (no pumping), the spiral terminates at the centre of the
sphere: the maximally mixed state. The spin has lost all information
about its initial state to the environment.

** Cross-domain map

This same sphere appears in:

- /NMR/: the nuclear spin in a static $B_0$ field, tipped by an rf
  pulse, precessing and relaxing. The free induction decay is the
  transverse projection of this spiral.

- /Quantum computing/: a qubit on the Bloch sphere, rotated by gates.
  The $\pi/2$ pulse in NMR is the Hadamard gate in a rotated frame.

- /Classical optics/: the Poincar\eacute{} sphere for polarisation states.
  The Stokes parameters $(S_1, S_2, S_3)$ are the Bloch vector for
  photon polarisation.

The structure is identical. The physics differs. Recognising the
sphere in a new domain transfers all the geometric intuition you have
already built.

** Where the picture breaks

The Bloch sphere is /exact/ for spin-½ (two-level) systems. For
higher spins ($F > 1/2$), the state space is higher-dimensional and
cannot be faithfully represented as a sphere. The density matrix for
spin-$F$ has $(2F+1)^2 - 1$ real parameters — for $F = 2$ (the $^{87}$Rb
ground state), that is 24 parameters, not 3.

The Bloch equations in this lesson work because we treat the ensemble
of $F = 2$ atoms as an effective two-level system. This approximation
holds when the Zeeman splitting is linear and the pumping addresses
only two levels. When it breaks — at high fields, or with quadratic
Zeeman shifts — the Bloch sphere picture fails and we need the full
density matrix (Lesson 02).

The Landau expansion derives exactly where the approximation holds.
Here, the picture tells you what to expect before the derivation confirms it.
