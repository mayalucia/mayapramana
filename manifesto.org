* On Pramāṇa

Dignāga, writing fifteen centuries ago in a monastery that no longer
stands, asked a question that still has no final answer: what counts
as valid cognition?

He allowed only two paths. /Pratyakṣa/ --- direct perception, raw,
non-conceptual, free from the overlay of name and category. The sensor
meets the field. The photon strikes the detector. The atom fluoresces.
No interpretation yet, just contact.

And /anumāna/ --- inference. The mind takes the raw impression and
builds structure: this pattern means that cause; this signal implies
that field strength; this fluorescence tells me the detuning is
three linewidths off.

Dharmakīrti, a century later, sharpened the blade. Valid cognition is
not merely /correct/ --- it must be /reliably produced/. You cannot
stumble into knowledge by accident. The method matters. The causal
chain from world to sensor to inference must be sound at every link.
He called his criterion /arthakriyāsāmarthya/ --- the capacity for
effective action. Knowledge that does not enable you to /do/ something
is not knowledge at all.

A quantum sensor controller sits exactly at this boundary. It is the
apparatus that transforms pratyakṣa (the raw signal) into anumāna
(the calibrated measurement) --- and it must do so /reliably/, through
a causal chain that can be inspected, tested, and verified at every
step.

MāyāPramāṇa: the valid cognition of the measured world.

* On Measurement

#+begin_quote
As far as the laws of mathematics refer to reality, they are not
certain; and as far as they are certain, they do not refer to reality.
--- Albert Einstein, Oxford, 1933
#+end_quote

Hume would have appreciated quantum mechanics. He spent his life
arguing that we never /perceive/ causation --- we only perceive
constant conjunction. Events follow events; the mind supplies the
causal glue. The Problem of Induction: experience alone offers no
justification for trusting experience.

Then along comes Heisenberg, and the problem is no longer
philosophical but physical. The act of measurement disturbs the
measured. The observer is not a transparent window onto reality but a
participant who co-determines the outcome. Bohr saw this and chose
as his coat of arms the yin-yang symbol with the motto: /Contraria
sunt complementa/. Opposites are complementary.

#+begin_quote
It is wrong to think that the task of physics is to find out how
Nature is. Physics concerns what we can say about Nature.
--- Niels Bohr, as recorded by Aage Petersen
#+end_quote

Every quantum sensor embodies this insight. The magnetometer does not
/find/ the magnetic field. It participates in a dance between the
atomic spin and the external field, mediated by light, shaped by
feedback, and the number that comes out is not the field itself but
/what we can say/ about the field given our apparatus, our noise
floor, our integration time, our model.

The Cramér-Rao bound sets the speed limit on this knowledge. Fisher
information is the curvature of the likelihood --- the sharpness of
the peak that tells you how distinguishable two nearby field values
are. The bound says: no unbiased estimator can do better than the
inverse of the Fisher information.

This is not a limitation. It is the /grammar/ of measurement. And a
universal sensor controller must speak this grammar fluently.

* On the Universal Controller

#+begin_quote
/Pothi padhi padhi jag mua, pandit bhaya na koye/
/Dhai akhar prem ka, padhe so pandit hoye/

Reading books, the whole world died --- none became wise.
Whoever reads the two-and-a-half letters of Love, only they
become wise.
--- Kabir
#+end_quote

Kabir was not against books. He was against the confusion of /having
read/ with /having understood/. The same confusion plagues
instrument control.

Every quantum sensor lab builds its controller from scratch. LabVIEW
here, a custom FPGA there, Python scripts held together with hope
and grad student labour. Each controller works for one instrument,
one configuration, one set of assumptions. The knowledge is trapped
in the wiring, illegible, unreproducible.

A universal controller would be something different. Not a piece of
hardware but a /grammar/ --- a way of expressing what any quantum
sensor needs:

- *Feedback loops*: PID, Kalman filtering, lock-in detection ---
  the anumāna that transforms raw signal into calibrated measurement
- *Pulse sequences*: the choreography of light and rf that interrogates
  the quantum state --- the structured pratyakṣa
- *State estimation*: the Bayesian machinery that maintains a belief
  about the system state given noisy observations --- Dharmakīrti's
  reliable cognition, expressed in mathematics
- *Noise models*: shot noise, technical noise, quantum projection
  noise --- the /limits/ of what can be known, honestly accounted for

The same grammar should describe an atomic magnetometer, an NV-diamond
sensor, a gravimeter, an optical clock. The physics differs; the
epistemology does not. In each case: a quantum system is prepared,
interrogated, and measured, and the result is an inference about
the world, bounded by fundamental limits.

#+begin_quote
Physical concepts are free creations of the human mind, and are not,
however it may seem, uniquely determined by the external world.
--- Albert Einstein
#+end_quote

The controller is a /concept/ --- a free creation that organises the
dialogue between sensor and world. But it is not arbitrary. It is
constrained by what works, by the Cramér-Rao bound, by the physics
of the sensor, by the noise floor of the universe.

* On Digital Twins

#+begin_quote
What I cannot create, I do not understand.
--- Richard Feynman, last blackboard, February 1988
#+end_quote

How do you build a universal controller? Not by trying to abstract
across instruments you haven't understood. By building /digital twins/
of specific instruments, one at a time, until the common structure
emerges.

An atomic magnetometer: model the Bloch equations, the optical
pumping cycle, the magnetic resonance. Simulate the feedback loop.
Inject noise. Ask: does the Kalman filter converge? Does the PID
hold lock? What happens when the field changes faster than the
bandwidth?

Then an NV-diamond sensor: different physics (solid state, not atomic
gas), different noise (lattice vibrations, charge state fluctuations),
different readout (photoluminescence, not absorption). Same questions:
does the controller hold? Does the estimation converge? Where are the
limits?

The common structure does not come from abstraction imposed from
above. It comes from reconstruction practised from below --- from
building enough digital twins that the universals reveal themselves.

#+begin_quote
/Jaise til mein tel hai, jyon chakmak mein aag/
/Tera sayeen tujh mein hai, tu jaag sake to jaag/

As sesame contains oil, as flint contains fire ---
Your Lord is within you. Awaken, if you can.
--- Kabir
#+end_quote

The universal is already inside the particular. The grammar of
measurement is already inside each instrument. The digital twin is
the practice of awakening to it.

This is Markram's radical hypothesis, transported from neuroscience
to metrology: in any complex system, all parameters are
interdependent. Lay down a few anchor measurements and the rest are
constrained --- provided you have the right models to capture the
interdependencies. The digital twin is those models, made explicit,
made runnable, made testable.

* On Verification

#+begin_quote
Science is the belief in the ignorance of experts.
--- Richard Feynman, address to the National Science Teachers Association, 1966
#+end_quote

We do not trust our models because we are clever. We trust them
because we test them. Every quantitative claim about the digital
twin --- the bandwidth, the sensitivity, the lock range, the noise
floor --- must be backed by a test that runs, that passes, that can
be inspected by anyone.

In MāyāLucIA, we have built a prototype of /verified manuscripts/ ---
documents where every claim carries an inline badge linking to the
test that proves it. We propose the same standard for MāyāPramāṇa.
The digital twin of a magnetometer is not a paper. It is a repository
with tests. The sensitivity claim is not a sentence in an abstract.
It is a function that computes the Allan deviation from simulated data
and compares it to the Cramér-Rao bound.

If Dharmakīrti were alive today, he would not ask "is your model
correct?" He would ask: "does your model enable effective action?
Can you use it to /control/ the instrument? Does the controller that
emerges from the digital twin actually work?"

/Arthakriyāsāmarthya/. The capacity for effective action. That is
the test.

* On Information

#+begin_quote
How wonderful that we have met with a paradox. Now we have some hope
of making progress.
--- Niels Bohr
#+end_quote

There is a deep connection --- deeper than analogy --- between quantum
measurement and machine learning. Both are problems of inference
under uncertainty. Both are bounded by information-theoretic limits.
Both involve an agent (biological, electronic, or algorithmic) that
must extract structure from noise.

Fisher information appears in both: as the precision limit of a
quantum sensor, and as the curvature of the loss landscape in a
neural network. The Cramér-Rao bound limits the magnetometer; the
bias-variance tradeoff limits the estimator. Shannon entropy measures
the capacity of a communication channel; von Neumann entropy measures
the mixedness of a quantum state.

These are not coincidences. They are manifestations of the same
underlying mathematics: the geometry of probability distributions,
the structure of inference, the physics of information.

A course on information theory that uses LLMs as a prime example ---
as a system that compresses, generates, and transforms information
--- is not a pedagogical gimmick. It is a recognition that the same
Fisher information that bounds your magnetometer's sensitivity
also shapes the loss landscape of the model generating this text.

/Foota kumbh jal jal hi samaya, yahi tathya kathyo gyani/

Information inside the sensor. The sensor inside the information
field. The distinction between instrument and phenomenon dissolves
at the quantum limit. Kabir saw this. Bohr saw this. The mathematics
sees this.

* On Friendship and Physics

#+begin_quote
The supreme task of the physicist is to arrive at those universal
elementary laws from which the cosmos can be built up by pure
deduction. There is no logical path to these laws; only intuition,
resting on sympathetic understanding of experience, can reach them.
--- Albert Einstein
#+end_quote

Einstein said /sympathetic understanding/. Not analytic. Not
computational. /Sympathetic/ --- feeling with, feeling into, the kind
of knowing that comes from long acquaintance with a problem, from
having lived with it until it becomes part of your body's sense of
the world.

This is what twenty years of physics gives you. Not facts. Not
techniques. A /nose/ for when something is wrong. The kinesthetic
sense that this model is too smooth, this measurement too clean, this
inference too confident.

No AI has this. Not yet. Perhaps not ever. What AI has is speed,
breadth, tireless attention. It can traverse code-space and
concept-space faster than any physicist. It can hold ten thousand
parameters in working memory. It can generate and test hypotheses
at the speed of silicon.

The collaboration between a physicist who can smell wrongness and
a machine that can search exhaustively is not a compromise. It is
a /complementarity/ --- Bohr's word, Bohr's insight, applied to the
practice of science itself.

* What MāyāPramāṇa Will Be

A repository. Open. Tested. Verified.

- *Digital twins* of quantum sensors: atomic magnetometer first,
  then NV-diamond, then whatever comes next
- *A controller grammar*: the common language of feedback, estimation,
  and calibration across sensor types
- *Verified models*: every sensitivity curve, every noise floor, every
  bandwidth claim backed by a test
- *Connection to MāyāLucIA*: the same methodology of sparse-to-dense
  reconstruction, the same insistence on verification, the same
  philosophical commitment to understanding through creation

The Nyāya school, rivals of the Buddhists, admitted four pramāṇas
where Dignāga admitted two. They added /upamāna/ (analogy --- learning
about one system by comparing it to another) and /śabda/ (reliable
testimony --- trusting the teacher). In building digital twins across
sensor types, we practice upamāna: the magnetometer teaches us about
the gravimeter by analogy of structure. And in publishing the code
openly, with tests, we offer śabda: testimony that you can verify for
yourself, because the tests are right there next to the claims.

Perhaps the Naiyāyikas were right to keep all four.

MāyāPramāṇa does not resolve this entanglement. It inhabits it.
